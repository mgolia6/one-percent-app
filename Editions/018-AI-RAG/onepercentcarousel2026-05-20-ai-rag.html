What is RAG?
RAG gives a language model access to external knowledge at the moment it generates a response. Instead of relying on what was baked in during training, the system retrieves relevant documents or data first — then grounds the answer in what was actually found.
The core problem it solves: LLMs have a knowledge cutoff. They know what they were trained on, nothing more. Your company's internal policies, last quarter's data, a contract from last week — none of it exists to the model unless RAG brings it in.

AI · How It Works
018 · AI.3.1
The Mechanics
Three steps happen between your question and the model's answer:
1
Embed. Your question is converted into a vector — a mathematical representation of meaning, not just keywords.
2
Retrieve. The system searches a vector database of your actual documents for the most semantically relevant chunks.
3
Generate. Those chunks are fed to the model alongside your question. The answer is grounded in what was retrieved — not what the model vaguely remembers.
Introduced by Patrick Lewis et al. at Meta AI — NeurIPS 2020. The original paper showed RAG produces more specific, diverse, and factual outputs than parametric-only models.

AI · In the Wild
018 · AI.3.1
Type B · Documented Pattern
RAG as a trust architecture
A legal team deployed an AI assistant to surface case history. Demos were impressive. In production: hallucinated citations, wrong holdings, confident inaccuracies.
The model had no access to the firm's actual database. It was generating from memory — and didn't know what it didn't know.
After rebuilding with RAG: hallucinations dropped significantly. Every answer cited a retrievable source. Attorneys could verify, not just trust.
RAG isn't a performance upgrade. It's a trust architecture — the difference between AI that sounds right and AI that is right.
